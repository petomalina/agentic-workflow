# Intents

Braindump has two sides to every interaction: the **user's intent** (what they
are trying to do) and the **agent's intent** (what it must do in response).
Everything is mediated through the chat — there are no forms, no manual editing.

## User intents

- **Dump a braindump.** "I was at DevConf. Met Ada, we talked about recall."
  Capture people, what was discussed, when, and who else was there.
- **Add more, conversationally.** Follow one message with another — "I also
  talked to the speaker about decidability" — without re-stating context.
- **Label and organize.** "Tag Ada as a mentor." Ask the agent to categorize
  people.
- **Recall.** "Who did I meet about search?" "When did I last see Grace?"
  "What did we discuss?"
- **Track commitments.** "Remind me I owe Ada the architecture doc."

## Agent intents

The agent is **LLM-based** and **tool-using**. The user wants real language in,
real language out — so the agent must understand intent, not match keywords.

1. **Understand.** Parse the braindump for entities: people, the event/meeting,
   its context (the conference or call), other attendees, and any labels.
2. **Ask to enrich.** When useful for rapport, ask a clarifying question —
   *what challenge were they trying to solve?*, *what interesting thing did you
   learn about them?* — so the record is more than a name.
3. **Write, via tools.** Use database tools to create/update people, events,
   attendees, labels, and follow-ups. Editing the store is always the agent's
   job, never a form.
4. **Confirm correctness.** Reflect back what it captured ("Logged a coffee chat
   with Ada at DevConf, tagged mentor") so the user can correct it in chat.
5. **Search to answer recall.** For recall intents, query the database with
   tools and answer in natural language.

## Why it matters

The whole point is **rapport**: people build better relationships when they
remember the details. The agent's job is to lower the cost of remembering to
near zero — you just talk, it keeps the thread.
